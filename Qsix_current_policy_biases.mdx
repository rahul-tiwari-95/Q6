# Q6 Current Policy Biases and Architecture

Q6 is a compact research lab for a hard reinforcement learning problem: how to train an agent that must pursue a useful objective while surviving against a co-evolving adversary.

The visible game is simple: Krishna collects pellets in a 25x25 gridworld while Hunter chases. The actual research problem is deeper:

> Can a learned policy balance mission progress, survival, exploration, and adversarial adaptation when the opponent is also learning?

This document is meant to be a high-level entry point for anyone trying to understand what Q6 is, why it matters, where the current failure modes are, and what should be improved next.

## One-paragraph summary

Q6 began as a DQN gridworld agent trained against scripted Hunters. That worked: Krishna learned to collect pellets and evade predictable enemies. The project became interesting when Hunter was made into a learned adversary through self-play. At that point, Krishna discovered a rational but unwanted strategy: avoid risk, survive, and stop collecting. The current Q6 research thread is about breaking that policy bias using Fictitious Self-Play, historical opponent pools, a gated evade/collect network, and counterfactual hindsight replay.

## What Q6 is not

Q6 is not just a Pac-Man clone. It is also not yet a globally novel RL algorithm in the academic sense.

Its individual pieces are built from known RL ideas:

| Component | Prior art |
|---|---|
| DQN | Mnih et al., "Human-level control through deep reinforcement learning" |
| Double DQN | van Hasselt et al., "Deep Reinforcement Learning with Double Q-learning" |
| Dueling heads | Wang et al., "Dueling Network Architectures for Deep Reinforcement Learning" |
| Fictitious Self-Play | Heinrich and Silver, "Deep Reinforcement Learning from Self-Play in Imperfect-Information Games" |
| HER | Andrychowicz et al., "Hindsight Experience Replay" |
| Rainbow baseline | Hessel et al., "Rainbow: Combining Improvements in Deep Reinforcement Learning" |

The value of Q6 is the combination, the instrumentation, and the way it exposes a real RL failure mode in a small environment.

## The research question

Q6 asks:

> What happens when the reward function says "collect pellets" but the adversarial training distribution says "collection is dangerous"?

The current answer is:

> The agent learns to survive instead of completing the task, because survival has better expected value under the learned opponent distribution.

That is the core policy bias in Q6.

## Policy bias definition

In this project, a policy bias is an emergent preference produced by the combination of:

1. Reward weights
2. Opponent strength
3. Replay-buffer composition
4. Exploration schedule
5. Curriculum timing
6. Synthetic or counterfactual training data

The bias is not necessarily a bug in the neural network. Often it is the mathematically correct behavior under the incentives the project created.

## The central policy bias: survival over objective

Krishna is supposed to collect pellets. In self-play, Krishna often learns something else:

> Do not collect. Stay alive. Timeout safely.

This is not random failure. It is a reward-and-distribution failure.

Against a strong Hunter, a pellet attempt can be negative expected value:

```text
E[collect] = P(survive attempt) * pellet_reward
           + P(caught) * catch_and_loss_cost

E[evade]   = P(survive timeout) * survival/shaping reward
```

If collection requires moving toward dangerous positions and evasion avoids large penalties, then evasion wins.

This is the same pattern that appears in many real RL systems:

| Toy behavior in Q6 | Real-world analogue |
|---|---|
| Avoid pellets because Hunter is dangerous | Robot avoids useful tasks because collision penalties dominate |
| Timeout safely | Agent learns inactivity as the safest policy |
| Overfit to scripted Hunter | Policy memorizes environment quirks instead of general strategy |
| Strong Hunter pool kills collection | Curriculum advances too fast and removes safe practice |
| CHER props up collection | Synthetic supervision helps but does not fix live incentives |

## Bias 1: scripted-opponent overfitting

Phase 1 worked very well against scripted/progressive Hunters. The full run reached a high win rate and strong pellet collection.

But a scripted opponent gives Krishna a predictable target distribution. Krishna can learn:

```text
Avoid the pattern of this Hunter.
```

That is different from learning:

```text
Collect while adapting to an intelligent opponent.
```

This is why Phase 1 success was necessary but not sufficient. The transition to self-play was the correct research move.

## Bias 2: hardened-opponent pool bias

Phase 2 introduced Fictitious Self-Play: Krishna sometimes plays against frozen historical Hunter snapshots.

The intended benefit:

```text
Train against diverse Hunters, not only the current Hunter.
```

The observed problem:

```text
The FIFO pool eventually fills with strong late-game Hunters.
Early weak Hunters are evicted.
Safe collection practice disappears.
```

Once all opponents are hard, the policy receives mostly "collection is dangerous" evidence. The replay buffer becomes dominated by evasion and catch-avoidance transitions.

That is why Phase 3 introduced a hierarchical opponent pool:

| Tier | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Easy tier | Permanent weak-opponent practice |
| Hard tier | Current strong-opponent robustness |

This is a good design move because it preserves both practice and pressure.

## Bias 3: replay-buffer evasion bias

Replay buffers do not store "truth"; they store the distribution of what the agent actually did.

If Krishna spends thousands of episodes evading, then the buffer says:

```text
Most states are evasion states.
Most rewards come from survival.
Most successful outcomes are non-collection timeouts.
```

The model then improves at the behavior it sees most.

This creates a loop:

```text
Evasion policy -> evasion episodes -> evasion buffer -> evasion gradients -> stronger evasion policy
```

That loop is the heart of the collection paralysis problem.

## Bias 4: single-head gradient conflict

In Phase 2, one policy head must learn both:

1. Move toward pellets.
2. Move away from Hunter.

Those are often opposite gradients.

When Hunter is far away, "move toward pellet" is good.
When Hunter is nearby, "move toward pellet" can be fatal.

A single advantage head must blend these regimes implicitly. Under pressure, it tends to learn the safer dominant behavior: evasion.

Phase 3's Gated Option Policy is a direct response:

| Head | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Evade head | Learn survival/chase-avoidance behavior |
| Collect head | Learn pellet-oriented behavior |
| Gate | Blend heads using context: Hunter distance, pellets remaining, lives |

This is not full options-based HRL yet, but it is a useful option-like decomposition.

## Bias 5: CHER dependency

Counterfactual HER tries to fix the missing collection signal.

It scans completed episodes and asks:

```text
Was Hunter far?
Was a pellet nearby?
Did Krishna miss a safe collect opportunity?
```

If yes, it injects a synthetic transition that rewards the action toward the pellet.

This helps, but it creates a new question:

> Is Krishna collecting because it learned the live objective, or because CHER keeps injecting teacher signals?

The latest Phase 3 run suggests CHER dependency remains high. CHER fired 237,511 times. The collection rate improved, but wins remained rare.

The key future metric should be:

```text
CHER dependency index = rolling CHER injections / max possible injections
```

Success would mean this number declines over time while real collection rises.

## Bias 6: off-dynamics counterfactuals

The current CHER design changes the action and reward but keeps the same `next_state`.

That is useful as a heuristic teacher signal, but it is not fully faithful to environment dynamics. In a real MDP:

```text
different action -> usually different next_state
```

This can teach the collect head useful preferences, but it can also introduce unrealistic Q-targets.

Future CHER should either:

1. Re-simulate the one-step counterfactual action in the environment, or
2. Reframe the method as goal/reward relabeling instead of action relabeling.

## Bias 7: reproducibility drift

The latest Phase 3 run manifest records the committed Git SHA, but Phase 3 files are currently untracked or modified in the working tree.

That means:

```text
experiment.json says "this run used commit X"
but commit X does not fully contain the code that produced the run.
```

For a research project, this is a serious but fixable issue.

Every training run should capture:

1. Git commit
2. Git branch
3. Dirty worktree flag
4. Diff patch or hash of uncommitted files
5. Exact CLI command
6. Seed
7. Dependency versions

The dashboard and logs are already strong. Reproducibility metadata should be brought up to the same standard.

## Architecture overview

Q6 has three major implementation eras.

### Phase 1: scripted-opponent foundation

Purpose:

```text
Can DQN learn this game at all?
```

Key traits:

| Area | Design |
|---|---|
| Environment | `HunterGridworld` |
| Opponent | Scripted/progressive Hunter |
| Agent | DQN / DQNv2 |
| Network | CNN + Double DQN + Dueling |
| Result | Strong performance against scripted opponent |

Phase 1 proved the base environment, state encoding, training loop, and dashboard pipeline.

### Phase 2: self-play and FSP

Purpose:

```text
Can Krishna learn against a co-evolving Hunter?
```

Key traits:

| Area | Design |
|---|---|
| Environment | `SelfPlayGridworld` |
| Agents | Krishna DQNv2 + Hunter DQNv2 |
| Training modes | Joint latest-vs-latest and FSP vs frozen Hunter |
| Opponent memory | FIFO `OpponentPool` |
| Result | Self-play works, but collection collapses |

Phase 2 discovered the central policy bias.

### Phase 3: GOP + CHER + hierarchical pool

Purpose:

```text
Can architecture and counterfactual data break collection paralysis?
```

Key traits:

| Area | Design |
|---|---|
| Krishna agent | `GatedDQNAgent` |
| Network | `GatedOptionNetwork` |
| Replay | `ContextReplayBuffer` |
| Synthetic teacher | `CounterfactualHER` |
| Opponent memory | `HierarchicalOpponentPool` |
| Result | More collection, but wins still rare |

Phase 3 is the most interesting research layer, but it still needs correctness fixes, ablation, and reward redesign.

## Important implementation caveat

The current `train_phase3.py` appears to contain a Hunter learning bug:

```python
hunter.step(
    state, a_k, rewards["hunter"], next_state, done or trunc
)
```

The second argument should likely be `a_h`, not `a_k`.

Phase 2 does this correctly:

```python
hunter.step(state, a_h, rewards["hunter"], next_state, done or trunc)
```

This matters because Hunter may be learning from Krishna's action labels during Phase 3. Any Phase 3 conclusion should be treated as provisional until this is fixed and re-run.

## Why Q6 is worth working on

Q6 is valuable because it makes several hard RL issues visible:

| Hard RL problem | How Q6 exposes it |
|---|---|
| Reward misspecification | Survival beats collection under current incentives |
| Non-stationarity | Hunter changes while Krishna learns |
| Curriculum fragility | Too-hard opponents destroy collection practice |
| Sparse success | Full 4-pellet wins become rare |
| Replay distribution bias | Evasion fills the buffer |
| Synthetic data risk | CHER helps but may become a crutch |
| Interpretability | Gate values, replays, logs, and dashboard expose behavior |

The project is worth thinking about because it is small enough to understand and large enough to show real RL pathologies.

## What would make Q6 stronger

### P0: Correctness and reproducibility

1. Fix the Phase 3 Hunter action bug.
2. Commit Phase 3 code, diagrams, docs, and dashboard updates.
3. Add dirty-worktree metadata to every experiment manifest.
4. Add a smoke test for Phase 3 training artifacts.
5. Add a regression test that Hunter learns from `a_h`, not `a_k`.

### P1: Scientific ablations

Run controlled comparisons:

| Variant | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Phase 2 baseline | Establish original collapse |
| GOP only | Does gating help without CHER? |
| CHER only | Does synthetic collection help without dual heads? |
| Hierarchical pool only | Does preserving easy opponents help? |
| GOP + CHER | Is the pair enough? |
| GOP + CHER + hierarchical pool | Full Phase 3 |

Each should run multiple seeds.

### P2: Reward redesign

The v6 docs point in the right direction: fix the incentive structure, not only the architecture.

Promising changes:

1. Penalize zero-pellet timeouts.
2. Reward safe collection windows used.
3. Add pellet proximity shaping only when Hunter distance is safe.
4. Make collection expected value positive against medium-strength Hunters.
5. Track risk-adjusted collection, not just raw reward.

The goal:

```text
Krishna should collect because live rewards make collection rational,
not because CHER repeatedly tells it to collect.
```

### P3: Stronger DQN baselines

Q6 already has Double DQN and dueling heads. The missing Rainbow-style components are:

1. Prioritized Experience Replay
2. n-step returns
3. NoisyNets
4. Distributional Q-learning / C51

These are valuable because rare pellet transitions and rare wins should be sampled more intelligently.

### P4: Better opponent populations

Replace or augment simple pool sampling with:

1. Reservoir sampling across all historical Hunters
2. Elo-style Hunter difficulty estimates
3. Cross-play matrix: Krishna checkpoint N vs Hunter checkpoint M
4. PSRO-style policy mixtures
5. Fixed evaluation suite of frozen Hunters

### P5: Standard API wrappers

Add PettingZoo-style multi-agent wrappers around `SelfPlayGridworld`.

Benefits:

1. Easier comparison with CleanRL and RL libraries
2. PPO/MAPPO baselines become easier
3. Standard evaluation tooling
4. More credible external reproducibility

## Real-world application analogues

Q6 maps to many real systems where a useful task competes with safety pressure.

| Q6 scenario | Real system |
|---|---|
| Collect pellets while avoiding Hunter | Robot completes tasks while avoiding humans/obstacles |
| Learned Hunter adapts | Dynamic adversary in cyber defense or pursuit/evasion |
| Safe timeout strategy | Autonomous system learns inactivity as safest behavior |
| Easy/hard opponent pool | Curriculum for autonomous vehicles or drones |
| CHER safe-window relabeling | Learning from near-misses in driving/robotics logs |
| Gated evade/collect policy | Safety mode vs task mode in embodied agents |

The broader lesson:

> Intelligent systems often learn the safest proxy behavior unless the reward design makes the intended mission rational.

## What would make Q6 paper-worthy

Q6 could become a credible research artifact if it demonstrates:

1. A clearly defined policy-bias failure mode.
2. A reproducible environment and benchmark suite.
3. Strong baselines: DQN, Rainbow-style DQN, PPO/MAPPO if possible.
4. Ablations showing which Phase 3 component matters.
5. Multi-seed statistical results.
6. Visual diagnostics: replay examples, gate dynamics, CHER dependency.
7. Generalization tests on unseen layouts and opponent populations.
8. A corrected and dynamics-consistent counterfactual replay method.

The paper angle would not be "we invented DQN for Pac-Man."

The stronger angle would be:

> Diagnosing and correcting survival-dominant policy bias in adversarial sparse-reward self-play.

## Final north star

The most important next question is not:

```text
Can Krishna win more often?
```

The better question is:

```text
Can Q6 produce a policy that collects because the live incentive structure
makes collection rational, while still preserving robust evasion against
a learned adversary?
```

If Q6 answers that cleanly, it becomes more than a gridworld project. It becomes a small but serious study of policy bias, reward design, and adversarial self-play.
